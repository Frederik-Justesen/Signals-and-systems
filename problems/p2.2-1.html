Problem 2.2-1

$\textbf{Question}:\\$ Show that $e^{-at}$ starting at $-\infty$ is neither an energy signal nor a power signal for any real number $a$. However, if $a$ is purely imaginary, show that it is a power signal with power $P_g = 1$ regardless of the value of $a$.

$\textbf{Answer}:\\$ If $g(t) = e^{-at}$ then $g^2(t) = e^{-2at}$. First, we compute the energy $$\begin{align} E_g &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-2at} \: \text{d}t = -\frac{1}{2a} \Big[ e^{-2at} \Big]_{-\infty}^{\infty} \\\\ &= \lim_{t\to\infty} -\frac{1}{2a} \Big( e^{-2at} - e^{2at} \Big) = \infty \quad \text{because} \quad e^{2at} \to \infty \quad \text{for} \quad t \to \infty \end{align}$$ For the power, we get $$\begin{align} P_g &= \lim_{T\to\infty} \frac{1}{T} \int_{-\frac{T}{2}}^{\frac{T}{2}} e^{-2at} \: \text{d}t = \lim_{T\to\infty} -\frac{1}{2aT} \Big[ e^{-2at} \Big]_{-\frac{T}{2}}^{\frac{T}{2}} \\\\ &= \lim_{T\to\infty} -\frac{1}{2aT} \Big( e^{-aT} - e^{aT} \Big) = \infty \quad \text{because} \quad e^{2aT} \quad \text{grows faster than} \quad \frac{1}{2aT} \quad \text{decays} \end{align} $$ So for any real number $a$, $g(t) = e^{-at}$ is neither an energy nor a power signal. $\\$ However, if $a=j\alpha$ we have $$ \begin{align}|g(t)| &= |e^{-j\alpha t}| = |\cos(\alpha t) -j\sin(\alpha t)| \\\\ &= \sqrt{\cos^2(\alpha t) + \sin^2(\alpha t)} = \sqrt{1} = 1 \Rightarrow |g(t)|^2 = 1 \end{align}$$ Now, the power becomes $$\begin{align} P_g &= \lim_{T\to\infty} \frac{1}{T} \int_{-\frac{T}{2}}^{\frac{T}{2}} 1 = \lim_{T\to\infty} \frac{1}{T} \left( \frac{T}{2} + \frac{T}{2} \right) \\\\ &= \lim_{T\to\infty} \frac{1}{T} \cdot T = 1\end{align} $$
