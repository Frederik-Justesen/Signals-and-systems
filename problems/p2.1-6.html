Problem 2.1-6

$\textbf{Question}:\\$ Find the power of a sinusoid $C\cos(\omega_0 t + \theta) $ by averaging the signal energy over one period $\frac{2\pi}{\omega_0}$ (rather than averaging over the infinitely large interval).

$\textbf{Answer}:\\$ To find the power of $g(t) = C\cos(\omega_0 t + \theta)$ we first find the energy. We note that $$g^2(t) = C^2\cos^2(\omega_0 t + \theta) = \frac{C^2}{2}\Big(1+\cos(2\omega_0 t + 2\theta) \Big)$$ Now, we carry out the energy integral over one period only (since $g(t)$ is periodic evaluating the integral from $-\infty$ to $\infty$ would not lead to a convergent result): $$\begin{align}E_g &= \int^{T_0}_0 g^2(t) \: \text{d}t = \int^{\frac{2\pi}{\omega_0}}_0 g^2(t) \: \text{d}t \\\\ &= \frac{C^2}{2} \Bigg[\int^{\frac{2\pi}{\omega_0}}_0 1 \: \text{d}t + \int^{\frac{2\pi}{\omega_0}}_0 \cos(2\omega_0 t + 2\theta) \: \text{d}t \Bigg] \\\\ &= \frac{C^2}{2} \Bigg[\frac{2\pi}{\omega_0} + \frac{1}{2\omega_0}\Big[\sin(2\omega_0 t + 2\theta) \Big]_0^{\frac{2\pi}{\omega_0}}\Bigg] \\\\ &= \frac{C^2}{2} \Bigg[\frac{2\pi}{\omega_0} + \frac{1}{2\omega_0}\Big[\sin(2\omega_0 \frac{2\pi}{\omega_0} + 2\theta) - \sin(2\theta) \Big]\Bigg] \\\\ &= \frac{C^2}{2} \Bigg[\frac{2\pi}{\omega_0} + \frac{1}{2\omega_0}\Big[\sin(4\pi + 2\theta) - \sin(2\theta) \Big]\Bigg] \\\\ &= \frac{C^2}{2} \Bigg[\frac{2\pi}{\omega_0} + \frac{1}{2\omega_0}\Big[\sin(2\theta) - \sin(2\theta) \Big]\Bigg] \\\\ &= \frac{C^2}{2}\frac{2\pi}{\omega_0} \\\\ &= \frac{C^2T_0}{2} \end{align}$$ Now we find the power by averaging the energy over one period. $$P_g = \frac{E_g}{T_0} = \frac{1}{T_0} \cdot \frac{C^2T_0}{2} = \frac{C^2}{2} $$ Hence, the power is just the half the amplitude squared.
