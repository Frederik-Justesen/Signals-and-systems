Problem 2.1-8

$\textbf{Question}:\\$ If $$g(t) = \sum_{k=m}^{n} D_k e^{j\omega_k t}, \quad D_k \in \mathbb{C}, \quad \omega_k \in \mathbb{R} $$ show that the associated power is $$P_g = \sum_{k=m}^{n} |D_k|^2 $$ This is called Parseval's theorem.

$\textbf{Answer}:\\$ For complex numbers, one has the identity $$|z|^2 = zz^\ast $$ Applying this to $g(t)$ we get $$\begin{align}|g(t)|^2 &= \sum_{k=m}^n D_ke^{j\omega_kt} \bigg[\sum_{r=m}^n D_r e^{j\omega_rt}\bigg]^\ast \\\\ &= \sum_{k=m}^n\sum_{r=m}^n D_kD_r^\ast e^{j\omega_kt} \big(e^{j\omega_rt}\big)^\ast \\\\ &= \sum_{k=m}^n\sum_{r=m}^n D_kD_r^\ast e^{j\omega_kt} e^{-j\omega_rt} \\\\ &= \sum_{k=m}^n\sum_{r=m}^n D_kD_r^\ast e^{j(\omega_k - \omega_r)t} \end{align}$$ Note that $\big(e^{j\omega_rt}\big)^\ast = e^{-j\omega_r t}$ because $\omega_r$ is real. Also note that we are allowed to group the summation signs, because both series are finite. $\\$ The power of $g(t)$ is $$P_g = \lim_{T\to \infty} \frac{1}{T} \int_{-\frac{T}{2}}^{\frac{T}{2}} \sum_{k=m}^n\sum_{r=m}^n D_kD_r^\ast e^{j(\omega_k - \omega_r)t} \: \text{d}t $$ This expression looks complicated, so let's investigate if we can simplify it. Let $\Delta \omega = \omega_k -\omega_r$, then $$\begin{align} P_g &= \lim_{T\to \infty} \frac{1}{T} \int_{-\frac{T}{2}}^{\frac{T}{2}} \sum_{k=m}^n\sum_{r=m}^n D_kD_r^\ast e^{j\Delta \omega t} \: \text{d}t \\\\ &= \sum_{k=m}^n\sum_{r=m}^n \lim_{T\to \infty} \frac{1}{T} D_kD_r^\ast \int_{-\frac{T}{2}}^{\frac{T}{2}} e^{j\Delta \omega t} \: \text{d}t \end{align}$$ We are allowed to swap the integral and the summation signs, because both summations are finite. Let's focus on the integral. There are two cases. $\\$ Case 1: $\Delta \omega \neq 0 \quad (k\neq r)$ $$\begin{align}\int_{-\frac{T}{2}}^{\frac{T}{2}} e^{j\Delta \omega t} \: \text{d}t &= \frac{1}{j\Delta\omega} \Big[ e^{j\Delta \omega t} \Big]_{-\frac{T}{2}}^{\frac{T}{2}} \\\\ &= \frac{1}{j\Delta\omega} \Big( e^{j\Delta \omega\frac{T}{2}} - e^{-j\Delta \omega \frac{T}{2}} \Big)\end{align}$$ Recall that $e^{j\theta} - e^{-j\theta} = 2j\sin(\theta)$: $$\frac{1}{j\Delta\omega} \Big( e^{j\Delta \omega\frac{T}{2}} - e^{-j\Delta \omega \frac{T}{2}} \Big) = \frac{2j\sin(\Delta \omega \frac{T}{2})}{j\Delta \omega} $$ Notice that this is a finite term, and that as $T\to \infty$ the power becomes zero: $$\lim_{T\to \infty} \frac{1}{T}D_kD_r^\ast\frac{2j\sin(\Delta \omega \frac{T}{2})}{j\Delta \omega} = 0 $$ Hence, we can ignore the cases when $\omega_k \neq \omega_r$ in the power calculation. $\\$ Case 2: $\Delta \omega = 0 \quad (k=r) \\$ Here, the integral yields $$\int_{-\frac{T}{2}}^{\frac{T}{2}} D_kD_k^\ast e^{0} \: \text{d}t = |D_k|^2T$$ The power thus becomes $$\lim_{T\to \infty} \frac{1}{T} |D_k|^2T = |D_k|^2$$ So only when $k=r$ does the power yield a non-zero result. Hence, we should only look at these cases. The double summation turns into a single summation, and the term inside becomes $ \lim_{T\to \infty} \frac{1}{T}|D_k|^2T$. $$\begin{align} P_g &= \sum_{k=m}^n\sum_{r=m}^n \lim_{T\to \infty} \frac{1}{T} D_kD_r^\ast \int_{-\frac{T}{2}}^{\frac{T}{2}} e^{j\Delta \omega t} \: \text{d}t\\\\ &= \sum_{k=m}^n \lim_{T\to \infty} \frac{1}{T} |D_k|^2T \\\\ &= \sum_{k=m}^n |D_k|^2 \end{align} $$
