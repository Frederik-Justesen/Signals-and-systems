Power of $g(t) = A\cos(\omega_0 t + \theta)$

General formula for power of a real signal $$P_g = \lim_{T\to \infty} \frac{1}{T} \int_{\frac{T}{2}}^{\frac{T}{2}} |g(t)|^2 \: \text{d}t$$ Averaging $g^2(t)$ over an infinitely large interval is identical to averaging over one period $T_0 = \frac{2\pi}{\omega_0}$. $$P_g = \frac{1}{T_0} \int_{0}^{T_0} A^2\cos^2(\omega_0 t + \theta) \: \text{d}t$$ Using the trig identity $\cos^2 x = \frac{1 + \cos(2x)}{2}$ gives $$P_g = \frac{A^2}{2T_0} \int_{0}^{T_0} 1 + \cos(2\omega_0 t + 2\theta) \: \text{d}t$$ The integral of the constant 1 is simply $T_0$, and the integral of the cosine is zero since it completes an integer number of periods over $[0,T_0]$. $$P_g = \frac{A^2}{2T_0} \cdot T_0 = \frac{A^2}{2}$$ Thus, the power of a sinusoid is half its amplitude squared: $$\boxed{P_g = \frac{A^2}{2}}$$ Notice that the phase shift $\theta$ does not affect the power.
