Power of $g(t) = A\cos(\omega_0 t + \theta)$

General formula for power of a real signal $$P_g = \lim_{T\to \infty} \frac{1}{T} \int_{\frac{T}{2}}^{\frac{T}{2}} |g(t)|^2 \: \text{d}t$$ Because $g(t)$ is periodic, averaging $g^2(t)$ over an infinitely large interval is identical to averaging over one period $T_0 = \frac{2\pi}{\omega_0}$. $$P_g = \frac{1}{T_0} \int_{0}^{T_0} A^2\cos^2(\omega_0 t + \theta) \: \text{d}t$$ Using the identity $\cos^2 x = \frac{1 + \cos(2x)}{2}$ gives $$\begin{align}P_g &= \frac{A^2}{2T_0} \int_{0}^{T_0} 1 + \cos(2\omega_0 t + 2\theta) \: \text{d}t \\\\ &= \frac{A^2}{2T_0} \left( \int_{0}^{T_0} 1 \: \text{d}t + \int_{0}^{T_0} \cos(2\omega_0 t + 2\theta) \: \text{d}t \right) \\\\ &= \frac{A^2}{2T_0} \left( \Big[t \Big]_0^{T_0} + \frac{1}{2\omega_0} \Big[\sin(2\omega_0 t + 2\theta)\Big]_0^{\frac{2\pi}{\omega_0}} \right) \\\\ &= \frac{A^2}{2T_0} \left( T_0 + \frac{1}{2\omega_0} \Big[\sin(2\omega_0 \frac{2\pi}{\omega_0} + 2\theta) - \sin(2\theta)\Big] \right) \\\\ &= \frac{A^2}{2T_0} \left( T_0 + \frac{1}{2\omega_0} \Big[\sin(4\pi + 2\theta) - \sin(2\theta)\Big] \right) \\\\ &= \frac{A^2}{2T_0} \left( T_0 + \frac{1}{2\omega_0} \Big[\sin(2\theta) - \sin(2\theta)\Big] \right) \\\\ &= \frac{A^2}{2T_0} \left( T_0 + 0 \right) \\\\ &= \frac{A^2}{2T_0}T_0 \\\\ &= \frac{A^2}{2} \end{align}$$ Thus, the power of a sinusoid is half its amplitude squared: $$\boxed{P_g = \frac{A^2}{2}}$$ Notice that the phase shift $\theta$ does not affect the power.
