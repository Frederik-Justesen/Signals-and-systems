Power of $g(t) = A\cos(\omega_0 t + \theta) + B$

General formula for power of a real signal $$P_g = \lim_{T\to \infty} \frac{1}{T} \int_{\frac{T}{2}}^{\frac{T}{2}} |g(t)|^2 \: \text{d}t$$ Because $g(t)$ is periodic, averaging $g^2(t)$ over an infinitely large interval is identical to averaging over one period $T_0 = \frac{2\pi}{\omega_0}$. $$P_g = \frac{1}{T_0} \int_{0}^{T_0} A^2\cos^2(\omega_0 t + \theta) + 2AB\cos(\omega_0 t + \theta) + B^2 \: \text{d}t$$ From Power of sinusoid we already know that $\frac{1}{T_0} \int_{0}^{T_0} A^2\cos^2(\omega_0 t + \theta) = \frac{A^2}{2}$ and that $\frac{1}{T_0} \int_{0}^{T_0} 2AB\cos(\omega_0 t + \theta) \: \text{d}t = 0$. Thus, $$\begin{align} P_g &= \frac{A^2}{2} + \frac{1}{T_0}\int_{0}^{T_0} B^2 \: \text{d}t \\\\ &= \frac{A^2}{2} + B^2 \end{align}$$ Thus, the power of a sinusoid is just the half the amplitude squared plus the offset squared. The phase shift $\theta$ has no effect. $$\boxed{P_g = \frac{A^2}{2} + B^2}$$
